About Independence Day

Independence Day is celebrated annually on 15 August as a public holiday in India commemorating the nation's independence from the United Kingdom on 15 August 1947, the day when the provisions of the Indian Independence Act, which transferred legislative sovereignty to the Indian Constituent Assembly, came into effect. India retained King George VI as head of state until its transition to a republic, when the Constitution of India came into effect on 26 January 1950 (celebrated as Indian Republic Day) and replaced the dominion prefix, Dominion of India, with the enactment of the sovereign law Constitution of India. India attained independence following the independence movement noted for largely non-violent resistance and civil disobedience led by Indian National Congress under the leadership of Mahatma Gandhi who adopted these values from one of the early movements[1] in India led by Ram Singh Kuka (quoted in one of the letters by Shaheed Bhagat Singh referring Guru Ram Singh as his Dada Guru).



History Of India Independence

India's Independence Day, observed on August 15th, is a significant milestone in the country’s history, marking the end of British colonial rule that lasted for nearly two centuries. The journey to independence was long and complex, involving years of struggle, sacrifice, and determination by countless Indians who yearned for freedom.
The Colonial Era
The British East India Company initially came to India in the early 17th century for trade, but gradually expanded its control over large parts of the country through military force and strategic alliances. By the mid-19th century, after the Indian Rebellion of 1857 (also known as the First War of Independence), the British Crown took direct control of India, establishing the British.
The Struggle for Independence
The movement for Indian independence gained momentum in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It was marked by a series of uprisings, reforms, and movements led by figures such as Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Lala Lajpat Rai, Bipin Chandra Pal (the Lal-Bal-Pal trio), and later, leaders like Mahatma Gandhi, Jawaharlal Nehru, Subhas Chandra Bose, and Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel. Mahatma Gandhi's Role: One of the most influential leaders of the independence movement was Mahatma Gandhi, who advocated for nonviolent resistance (Satyagraha) against British rule. His strategies included boycotts of British goods, civil disobedience, and mass protests, which garnered widespread support across the nation.
